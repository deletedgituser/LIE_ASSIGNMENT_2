About Rizal

José Protacio Rizal Mercado y Alonzo Realonda

José Rizal (1861–1896) was a Filipino nationalist, writer, and revolutionary regarded as one of the greatest heroes of the Philippines. Born in Calamba, Laguna, Rizal was a polymath, excelling in multiple fields such as medicine, literature, and linguistics. He completed his medical studies in Europe, where he also became politically active in advocating for reforms in the Philippines, which was then a colony of Spain. Rizal wrote two influential novels, Noli Me Tangere (1887) and El Filibusterismo (1891), which exposed the social injustices and abuses of Spanish colonial rule. These works stirred a sense of nationalism among Filipinos and laid the groundwork for the Philippine Revolution against Spain. Although Rizal advocated for peaceful reform rather than violent revolution, his ideas were seen as subversive. He was arrested and executed by the Spanish authorities in 1896. His martyrdom ignited the revolution, eventually leading to Philippine independence. Rizal is celebrated for his intellect, patriotism, and his role in inspiring the Philippines' struggle for freedom.

Family Background

José Rizal was born to a well-off family in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines. His parents and siblings played significant roles in his life.

Francisco Mercado Rizal (1818–1898)

Father: A prosperous farmer and educated man, he instilled values of hard work and education in his children.

Teodora Alonso Realonda (1827–1911)

Mother: A highly educated woman, she was instrumental in Rizal's early education and fostered his love for literature.

Saturnina Rizal (1850–1913)

Eldest Sister: Known as "Neneng," she acted as a second mother to Rizal and supported his education.

Paciano Rizal (1851–1930)

Brother: A key figure in Rizal’s life and a revolutionary, he supported José's educational pursuits.

Narcisa Rizal (1852–1939)

Sister: Supported Rizal during his time in Europe and helped care for him in his exile.

Olympia Rizal (1855–1887)

Sister: Close to Rizal, she died at a young age.

Lucia Rizal (1857–1919)

Sister: Married Mariano Herbosa, her husband was denied a Catholic burial due to association with Rizal.

Maria Rizal (1859–1945)

Sister: A confidant of Rizal who supported him during his exile.

Concepcion Rizal (1862–1865)

Sister: Died at three years old; her death profoundly affected Rizal.

Josefa Rizal (1865–1945)

Sister: Unmarried and involved in the revolutionary movement.

Trinidad Rizal (1868–1951)

Sister: With Rizal during his final moments; he entrusted her with his last poem.

Soledad Rizal (1870–1929)

Youngest Sister: Married Pantaleon Quintero; also a schoolteacher.

His Works

José Rizal was a prolific writer and reformist whose works played a crucial role in the Philippine struggle for independence. His novels, essays, and poems highlighted the social injustices of his time and inspired a sense of nationalism among Filipinos. Through his literary contributions, Rizal sought to awaken the consciousness of his fellow countrymen and advocate for social reforms, making him a pivotal figure in Philippine history.
